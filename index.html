Acquiring fraud detection. Less friction. Less loss. More governance.

CyberAntifraud is a research hub for my PhD work at NOVA IMS on fraud detection in merchant acquiring. The core goal is practical and measurable. Reduce false positives that create customer and merchant friction, and reduce false negatives that translate into direct fraud losses. The work combines rigorous temporal validation, cost-aware evaluation, and operational AI governance suitable for regulated environments.

Data disclosure policy: no sensitive operational data is published. Public examples rely on synthetic or anonymised datasets.

Research focus

Research questions

  • How can we reduce false positives while preserving or improving fraud capture, under strict temporal validation?
  • How should we evaluate models when the real objective is net benefit, not accuracy?
  • How can we detect and control drift in acquiring, where merchant and attacker behaviour evolves continuously?
  • How can governance, auditability and human oversight be operationalised without killing performance?

Scope: card-present and e-commerce acquiring, merchant context, behavioural signals, and cost-aware decision policies.

Evaluation protocol

Temporal validation

Fraud data is non-stationary. Random splits are misleading. This work uses blocked time-series validation and rolling windows to approximate production reality, quantify drift sensitivity, and avoid leakage.

Download: [Protocol PDF link] • Code: [Repo link]

Cost-sensitive evaluation

The primary KPI is net benefit. False positives create friction and operational cost. False negatives create direct loss. The evaluation includes cost curves, threshold policies, and stability under drift, not just AUC.

Download: [Cost model note link] • Example: [Synthetic demo link]

Metrics tracked

  • Precision, Recall, AUPRC, and minority-focused metrics where applicable.
  • Cost-weighted loss and expected net benefit under operational constraints.
  • Stability across time: variance across windows, drift sensitivity, and calibration drift.
  • Operational metrics: alert volume, analyst load, and override rates.

Governance and ethics

Governance principles

  • Data minimisation and strict purpose limitation for anti-fraud.
  • Documented model lineage: data version, features, training window, and parameters.
  • Human oversight for high impact decisions, with clear accountability.
  • Auditability by design: logs, model cards, and decision traceability.

Operational explainability

Explainability is treated as an operational instrument, not a compliance checkbox. The goal is to enable analysts, risk teams and auditors to understand why a decision occurred, and how stable that reason is under drift.

Disclosure policy

No production secrets, sensitive rules, merchant identities, or personal data are published. Public materials use synthetic data or anonymised aggregates. Any public robustness demonstrations are designed to inform defence, not enable abuse.

AI governance framework for acquiring fraud

1. Risk register updates

  • AI-assisted social engineering and merchant onboarding fraud.
  • Deepfake voice and video fraud in call centres and identity checks.
  • Prompt injection and data poisoning in AI-enabled workflows.
  • Model leakage, misuse, and shadow AI usage by internal teams.

2. Control enhancements

  • Monitoring for drift, calibration decay, and adversarial shifts.
  • Secure AI integration: access control, logging, and input output filtering.
  • Model validation: bias testing, robustness tests, and release gates.
  • Human-in-the-loop for defined high impact thresholds.

3. AI usage policies

  • Acceptable use and prohibited data categories in prompts.
  • Ownership and accountability for AI outputs and decisions.
  • Third-party AI risk management and vendor controls.

4. Framework alignment

  • ISO 27001: AI as an information asset with security controls.
  • NIST RMF and AI RMF: risk assessment and AI-specific controls.
  • ISO 42001: AI management system across the lifecycle, including impact assessment and monitoring.
  • SOC 2: evidence of controls, logging, and continuous monitoring.
