What this PhD is solving

  • Acquiring fraud detection is non stationary. Offline results are routinely inflated by leakage and invalid splits.
  • False positives generate friction, operational load, and commercial damage. False negatives generate direct fraud loss and downstream cost.
  • The goal is not accuracy. The goal is expected net benefit under realistic temporal deployment constraints.
  • The contribution is an evaluation and governance stack that makes claims defensible, audit ready, and operationally relevant.

Scope and non scope

  • Scope. Merchant acquiring, card present and e-commerce scenarios, merchant context signals.
  • Scope. Time aware evaluation, drift sensitivity, cost aware decision policies.
  • Non scope. Publishing proprietary rules, merchant identities, or operational secrets.
  • Non scope. Any public release of raw production data.

Evaluation stance

  • Temporal splits only. No random split claims.
  • Blocked time series cross validation plus rolling window backtests.
  • Leakage prevention as a first class requirement, with explicit feature time rules.
  • Metrics reported as a bundle. AUPRC, recall at constrained precision, cost curves, calibration and stability.
  • Decision policy framed by expected net benefit and operational capacity.

If a result looks too good, assume leakage until proven otherwise. This project treats that as default scepticism.

Governance stance

  • Data minimisation and purpose limitation for anti fraud.
  • Traceability. Dataset window, features, versioning, training and inference lineage.
  • Release gates. Temporal backtests, drift canaries, rollback criteria.
  • Human oversight for high impact decisions with explicit accountability.

Limitations and risks

  • Public artefacts are synthetic or anonymised. Production data remains confidential by design.
  • Cost assumptions are scenario based and require sensitivity analysis across plausible ranges.
  • Attacker adaptation and merchant evolution impose drift. Continuous monitoring is not optional.
  • Operational constraints can dominate optimal thresholds. Capacity constraints must be explicit.

What I want from a reviewer

  • Is the problem framing tight and defensible for a Q1 venue, especially around net benefit and temporal realism?
  • Is the evaluation protocol credible and sufficiently strict to prevent inflated claims?
  • Are limitations explicit enough, and are there missing threats to validity?
  • Is the governance stance practical, not performative, and aligned with regulated operations?

Three concrete flaws that would cause rejection and how to fix them is the highest value feedback.
